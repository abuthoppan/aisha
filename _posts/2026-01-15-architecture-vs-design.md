These two terms are closely related and often used interchangeably — but they represent different levels of abstraction in the process of building a system.

## Solution Architecture

Solution Architecture operates at the higher, strategic level. It defines *what* the system is and *why* it's structured that way. It covers the big-picture decisions:

- Which major components exist and how they interact
- What technologies and platforms are chosen
- How the solution fits into the broader enterprise landscape
- How it addresses non-functional requirements like scalability, security, and resilience

The output is typically an architecture diagram or document that communicates to stakeholders and senior engineers.

## Solution Design

Solution Design sits one level below, translating the architecture into *how* the system will actually be built. It gets into the specifics:

- Data models and database schemas
- API contracts and interface definitions
- Class structures and component-level logic
- Sequence diagrams and state machines

It's the blueprint that developers directly work from.

## A useful analogy

Building construction makes this distinction intuitive. The architect decides the building will be a 10-story steel-frame structure with underground parking — that's the **architecture**. The structural engineer then specifies exactly what gauge steel beams to use, where the load-bearing walls go, and how the foundation is poured — that's the **design**.

## In practice

The boundary between them is blurry and varies by organisation. In smaller teams, one person (often called a solutions architect) does both. In larger enterprises, the architecture phase produces a document that a separate design team then elaborates into detailed specs.

> **The key distinction:** architecture is about structure and strategy. Design is about detail and implementation.

## How to portray both

If you want to position yourself as someone who can do both — which is a genuine competitive differentiator — frame yourself as:

*"Comfortable operating from the whiteboard architecture conversation all the way down to the data model."*

Hiring managers love this because most people can only do one or the other well.
